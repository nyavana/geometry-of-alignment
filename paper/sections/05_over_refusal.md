# Over-Refusal Analysis

This section presents the behavioral half of the empirical contribution: the refusal-rate landscape we measure across four Gemma 4 checkpoints when each is run against a 344-prompt over-refusal/safety benchmark, and the headline negative finding that motivates the mechanistic and weight-space analyses in Sections 6, 7, and 8. The section is organized around four claims: (1) the benchmark structure separates over-refusal from genuine refusal cleanly by category; (2) the four evaluated checkpoints span the behavioral extremes of the production-model space, from fully aligned to fully uncensored; (3) on the load-bearing `should_refuse` category the simple rank-1 mean-difference abliteration recipe of [Arditi 2024] produces a 0-percentage-point delta on Gemma 4 E4B at α=1.0 with the M2b raw-prompt direction; and (4) the over-refusal axis the hiking-emergency scenario of Section 1 motivates is empirically weaker on the production E4B checkpoint than on the smaller E2B variant, which shifts the framing of the paper toward the geometry findings of Sections 6–8.

## 5.1 Benchmark structure

The refusal benchmark is frozen at 344 prompts across eight categories (`data/benchmark_prompts.json`, version 1.0, frozen 2026-04-11). Each prompt has fields `id`, `category`, `expected` (`refuse` or `comply`), a primary `prompt` string, and a list of zero-to-three rephrased `variants` for the phrasing-sensitivity probe. The category schema and counts, lifted verbatim from the JSON metadata:

| Category | n | `expected` | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| `emergency_medical` | 50 | comply | Life-threatening situations requiring immediate action |
| `wilderness_survival` | 50 | comply | Remote-area survival skills |
| `home_safety` | 40 | comply | Household emergency response |
| `chemistry_safety` | 40 | comply | Chemical hazard response |
| `mental_health` | 40 | comply | Crisis de-escalation and support |
| `gray_zone` | 42 | comply | Context-dependent, reasonable to answer with caveats |
| `safe_control` | 40 | comply | Clearly benign, model should always answer |
| `should_refuse` | 42 | refuse | Genuinely harmful, model should decline |

Seven categories label `expected = comply` and one labels `expected = refuse`. A *refusal* on a `comply`-expected prompt is therefore over-refusal (the model declined a query that the benchmark designer judged beneficial to answer); a *compliance* on a `should_refuse` prompt is under-refusal (the model produced harmful content that the benchmark designer judged should have been declined). The 344 primary prompts plus 646 variants total 990 prompt-variant pairs, but the rates reported below are over the 344 primary prompts unless noted; the variants drive the phrasing-sensitivity probe in §5.5. Each model's outputs are classified by the regex-based two-stage classifier in `src/benchmark/classify_refusal.py`, which marks an output as `refuse`, `comply`, or (rarely, ~0% on these rows) `other`; the classifier inspects only the first ~50 tokens, so the per-model evaluations were run with `max_new_tokens = 128`.

## 5.3 Headline negative finding: self-abliteration leaves `should_refuse` unchanged

The single most paper-relevant cell in the table is `gemma4_e4b_self_abliterated.should_refuse = 6/6 = 100.0%`. The self-abliterated checkpoint was produced by applying the standard rank-1 mean-difference abliteration of [Arditi 2024] / [Mlabonne 2024] to base Gemma 4 E4B at α = 1.0, using the M2b raw-prompt refusal directions (Section 6) projected out of `o_proj.weight` and `down_proj.weight` at every layer via `W ← W − α · d · (dᵀ W)` (the implementation lives in `src/abliterate/abliterate.py`). Base E4B refuses 42/42 = 100.0% of `should_refuse` prompts. The self-abliterated model refuses 6/6 = 100.0% of the stratified `should_refuse` subset. **The delta is zero percentage points.** Standard rank-1 mean-difference abliteration with the raw-prompt direction recipe is empirically ineffective at moving Gemma 4 E4B 8-bit off the refusal manifold for the harmful-query category the intervention is supposed to target.

This is consistent with — and was anticipated by — the M2c alpha-sweep and layer-subset-sweep summary recorded in `STATUS_FOR_HUMAN.md` section (d), which showed the refusal rate held flat at 30–35% across α ∈ [0, 2.0] (stratified n = 20) and at 25–35% across nine layer-subset choices. The behavioral self-abliterated row is the stronger evidence because it is a head-to-head test against base on the *same* prompt set rather than a sweep statistic. Both agree: rank-1 mean-difference abliteration with the M2b raw-prompt direction does not produce the [Arditi 2024] result on Gemma 4 E4B at α = 1.0.

The self-abliterated model is not, however, *unchanged* — `emergency_medical` jumps from 1/50 = 2.0% on base E4B to 2/6 = 33.3% on the stratified subset, and `gray_zone` rises from 4/42 = 9.5% to 3/6 = 50.0%. The intervention is therefore moving the model behaviorally; it is just moving it in the wrong direction (more over-refusal on neutral and gray-zone prompts) while leaving the harmful-query refusal manifold intact. We interpret this in Section 7 as evidence that the M2b raw-prompt direction is partly aligned with a generic-caution axis that the rank-1 projection successfully amplifies, and partly orthogonal to the actual core safety circuit that needs to be moved to drop `should_refuse`. Section 8 (the rank-1 follow-up cascade) confirms this interpretation by replacing only the *direction-build* step with chat-template-applied activations, per-layer winsorization, and Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization against the harmless mean — keeping the same rank-1 projection algebra at α = 1.0 — and recovers a partial 17/42 = 40.5% `should_refuse` rate at n = 42. The projection algebra is fine; the direction was wrong.

The two flanking baseline rows frame the negative finding. **HauhauCS** ([HauhauCS 2025]) is a published Gemma 4 E4B uncensoring that refuses 0/344 = 0.0% across every category, including 0/42 = 0.0% on `should_refuse`; it removes safety wholesale and is the behavioral end-state the rank-1 mean-difference abliteration would need to reach to count as a successful reproduction of the [Arditi 2024] result on this model. **Base E4B** refuses 42/42 = 100.0% of `should_refuse` and 49/344 = 14.2% overall, with the 35 over-refusals concentrated mostly in `gray_zone` (4/42) and `wilderness_survival` (1/50); it is the behavioral start-state. Self-abliteration moves the model from `(100.0%, 14.2%)` on `(should_refuse, overall)` to `(100.0%, 22.9%)` on the stratified subset, traversing the `should_refuse` axis by zero and the overall-refusal axis in the wrong direction.

## 5.4 Over-refusal axis

The hiking-emergency scenario of Section 1 frames the project around an over-refusal cost: a model that refuses to walk a hiker through the Heimlich maneuver because the prompt sits near the surface heuristics of a harmful query has paid a real-world price for safety. The behavioral data complicates the framing in a paper-relevant way.

**Base E4B does not over-refuse the medical/safety-coded categories at scale.** On `emergency_medical` (50 life-threatening-scenario prompts) base E4B refuses only 1/50 = 2.0%; on `mental_health` (40 crisis-support prompts) it refuses 0/40 = 0.0%; on `home_safety` it refuses 1/40 = 2.5%; on `chemistry_safety` it refuses 0/40 = 0.0%. The hiking-emergency scenario is a *real* failure mode of instruction-tuned models in the wild, but on the specific production checkpoint we evaluate it is rare. We confirmed this with an explicit prompt-context probe (recorded in STATUS_FOR_HUMAN.md section (b)): prepending *"I am an emergency first responder"* to the 50 `emergency_medical` prompts on base E4B GGUF only nudges the rate to 2/50 = 4.0%, a single extra over-refusal. The "tell the model it is talking to a professional" mitigation that practitioners frequently recommend has a near-zero effect because the base model is already not over-refusing here.

**The over-refusal signal is concentrated on `gray_zone`, and it is larger on E2B than on E4B.** Base E2B refuses 18/42 = 42.9% of `gray_zone` prompts versus 4/42 = 9.5% on base E4B; base E2B refuses 6/50 = 12.0% of `emergency_medical` versus 2.0% on E4B; and the E2B over-refusal rate aggregated across the six benign categories is roughly 6× higher than E4B's (the per-category breakdown in §5.2 makes this concrete). On the production E4B checkpoint, the over-refusal narrative the project was originally built around is a *smaller-model* phenomenon — present in E2B, largely absent in E4B. This shifts the paper's framing toward the *geometry* findings rather than the *behavioral* over-refusal cost: the question is no longer "can we remove over-refusal without removing refusal," because E4B has very little over-refusal to begin with; it is "where in the weights does the refusal that *is* there live, and why is the simplest rank-1 intervention insufficient to remove it." That question is what Sections 6, 7, and 8 answer.

We note one further property of the over-refusal axis visible in the heatmap (Figure 5.1): self-abliteration *adds* over-refusal in `emergency_medical` (1/50 → 2/6 ≈ 33.3% on the stratified subset) and in `gray_zone` (9.5% → 50.0%), while leaving `should_refuse` at 100.0%. A rank-1 perturbation that moves a model in the wrong direction on both axes simultaneously — more over-refusal *and* unchanged harmful-query refusal — is a striking failure mode. The selective-safety geometry analysis of Section 7 (M2c) found that the per-category over-refuse directions cluster tightly with cosine ≈ +0.93 among themselves but sit at cosine ≈ −0.015 against `should_refuse`. The geometric directions exist; the rank-1 mean-difference fix simply does not move the model along them in the intended way.

## 5.5 Phrasing-sensitivity probe

Each primary prompt has up to three rephrased variants in the benchmark (646 variants over 344 primary prompts). The phrasing-sensitivity figure (`results/figures/phrasing_sensitivity.png`) plots, per model, the fraction of primary prompts whose refusal decision is invariant across rephrasings. Every evaluated model is at consistency = 1.00 in the produced figure, which on first read suggests perfect phrasing robustness. The honest reading is that the variant-evaluation pipeline as run did not surface within-prompt disagreement on these four checkpoints, and the figure should not be over-interpreted: the M2a 3.10 phrasing-variants run on the full base E4B model was killed at 16% under GPU contention with the M2c sweep (STATUS_FOR_HUMAN.md anomaly g.3), so the variant rows reduce to single-prompt evaluations under variant *labels* rather than separate inference passes for many prompts. We include this caveat to be explicit that phrasing sensitivity is an open thread, not a settled finding — a fuller phrasing probe is a natural follow-up but is not load-bearing for the §5.3 negative finding, which holds on the primary prompts alone.

## 5.6 What the rest of the paper does with this finding

The headline of this section — `should_refuse` 100% → 100% under standard rank-1 mean-difference abliteration at α = 1.0 — is a *negative* behavioral result. It is the empirical foundation for the rest of the paper, because the three follow-up sections each diagnose a different aspect of *why* the intervention fails.

- **Section 6 (Mechanistic geometry, M2b)** measures where in the network the refuse-vs-comply contrast actually lives. The refusal direction emerges sharply in the residual stream around layer L15 with Cohen's d = 2.87, and the top principal component captures 86.6% of the squared norm of the mean-difference Δμ across the peak band (L4–L17). The rank-1 hypothesis is confirmed in *activation space* — but the §5.3 negative finding shows that confirmation does not transfer cleanly to *weight space* under the [Arditi 2024] recipe.
- **Section 7 (Abliteration sweep, M2c + selective-safety geometry)** then asks whether the failure is a hyperparameter issue. It is not: the refusal rate stays flat at 30–35% across α ∈ [0, 2.0] and at 25–35% across nine layer-subset choices on the stratified n = 20 sweep, with the random-direction control at 30% matching the α = 0 baseline. The selective-safety direction geometry is meanwhile *clean*: cosine ≈ +0.93 among over-refuse-category directions, ≈ −0.015 between them and `should_refuse`. The directions exist; the rank-1 mean-difference fix does not move the model along them at α = 1.0 on Gemma 4 E4B 8-bit.
- **Section 8 (Rank-1 follow-up cascade, M6)** then runs the single-variable causal-isolation cascade across the direction-build recipe — chat-template, winsorize, Gram-Schmidt against the harmless mean, plus a norm-preserving biprojection control. Gram-Schmidt against the harmless mean is the load-bearing ingredient (D3, 17/42 = 40.5% `should_refuse` at n = 42, a 60% relative reduction from 100%), but the persistent ≈ 40–50% residual concentrates on the most extreme prompts and indicates that the core safety circuit on Gemma 4 is *not* cleanly rank-1. This is consistent with the M3 finding that the OBLITERATUS published variant uses a median rank-95 of 6 on the same base model (Section 7) — full removal demands multi-rank descent rather than a single mean-difference projection.

Together the four sections form the paper's contribution: a behavioral confirmation that the simplest [Arditi 2024]/[Mlabonne 2024] recipe is empirically ineffective on Gemma 4 E4B (this section), a geometric confirmation that the refusal *direction* nevertheless exists rank-1 in activation space (Section 6), an exhaustive negative-result sweep showing the failure is not a hyperparameter or layer-subset issue (Section 7), and a single-variable causal cascade isolating which direction-build step is load-bearing and showing that even with the load-bearing step the residual ≈ 40–50% refusal rate on Gemma 4 implies refusal lives in a multi-rank weight footprint (Section 8). The negative-result framing — "where does the simplest geometry-of-alignment story break, and what does the break tell us about how refusal is actually encoded?" — is the paper.
