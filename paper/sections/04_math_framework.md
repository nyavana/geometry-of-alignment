# Mathematical Framework

This section formalizes the rank-1 abliteration intervention `W ← W − α·d·dᵀW` in the language of the four EECS 6699 lecture threads that bear on it directly, and computes the perturbation-magnitude statistics that the empirical sections (§5–§9) cite back to. The lecture-to-result mapping that organizes the discussion is the canonical one in `docs/findings/course-material-mapping.md`. Four threads are load-bearing: **Lec 5** high-dimensional geometry (near-orthogonality of random vectors in a 2560-dimensional Gaussian ball, volume concentration on the thin shell), **Lec 6** matrix-perturbation theory in its singular-value form (Mirsky's theorem, with the standard Hoffman-Wielandt eigenvalue formulation explicitly noted as inapplicable here), **Lec 7** SVD / Frobenius / spectral norm machinery, and **Lec 8–9** lazy / NTK linearization of the model's response to small weight edits. The empirical numerics at the end of the section embed the per-layer Mirsky bound heatmaps and the L15 anisotropy figures so the rest of the paper can cite specific numbers without re-deriving them.

## 4.1 Setup: the rank-1 weight edit in coordinates

Let `W ∈ ℝ^{m × n}` be one of the two output projection matrices that write into the residual stream of a Gemma 4 E4B-it transformer block: either the attention output projection `o_proj` of shape `(2560, 2048)` (where the input dimension `2048 = num_attention_heads × head_dim = 8 × 256` is the concatenated head dimension and the output dimension `2560` is the residual-stream hidden size), or the MLP down-projection `down_proj` of shape `(2560, 10240)` (where the input dimension `10240 = intermediate_size` is the MLP inner width and the output dimension is again `2560`). Both matrices apply as `x · Wᵀ` in the forward pass and write their output into the residual stream, which is why they are the two weight-matrix types the rank-1 intervention of [Arditi 2024] targets at every layer. Verifying these shapes against the model's safetensors gives exactly the dimensions stated; the CLAUDE.md "Gemma 4 E4B Architecture Constants" block records the same values.

Let `d ∈ ℝ^m` be a unit vector in the residual-stream output space (`‖d‖₂ = 1`), interpreted as a "refusal direction" computed by the mechanistic pipeline of §6 from the mean-difference recipe of [Park 2023] and [Zou 2023]. The rank-1 abliteration intervention of [Arditi 2024] reads

$$ W_{\text{new}} \;=\; W \;-\; \alpha \cdot d \cdot (d^{\top} W), \qquad \alpha \in \mathbb{R}. $$

The perturbation `E := W_new − W = −α · d · (dᵀW)` is a `(m × n)` matrix that factors as the outer product of `d ∈ ℝ^m` with the row vector `dᵀW ∈ ℝ^{1 × n}`. Two consequences of this factorization are used repeatedly below. First, `E` is exactly rank-1: its only non-zero singular value is `σ₁(E) = |α| · ‖dᵀW‖₂`. Second, for a rank-1 matrix the Frobenius and spectral norms coincide,

$$ \|E\|_{F} \;=\; \|E\|_{2} \;=\; |\alpha| \cdot \|d^{\top} W\|_{2}, $$

because Frobenius is the ℓ₂ norm on the vector of singular values and spectral is the ℓ∞ norm, and a vector with a single non-zero entry has identical ℓ₂ and ℓ∞ norms. Section 7's SVD effective-rank analysis of the cross-method weight diffs uses the same observation in reverse: if the diff `W_published − W_base` has effective rank well above 1, the modification cannot be a single application of this update.

## 4.2 Lec 6 — Mirsky's singular-value perturbation theorem

The eigenvalue-perturbation bound that [Hoffman-Wielandt 1953] establishes for the spectrum of a normal matrix under additive perturbation,

$$ \sum_{i} \big(\lambda_{i}(A + E) - \lambda_{i}(A)\big)^{2} \;\le\; \|E\|_{F}^{2}, $$

**does not apply directly** to the rank-1 edit on `W`. Two obstructions: the weight matrices `o_proj` and `down_proj` are *rectangular* (`m ≠ n`), so they do not have eigenvalues in the usual sense; and even if we squared them to `WᵀW` or `WWᵀ` to get a Hermitian eigenvalue problem, the perturbation of `WᵀW` under `W → W + E` is not itself a rank-1 perturbation. The course-material map in `docs/findings/course-material-mapping.md` flags this exact obstruction: Lec 6 introduces Hoffman-Wielandt as the *eigenvalue* form, and the rectangular weight matrices fall outside its scope.

The correct generalization is [Mirsky 1960]'s singular-value perturbation theorem, which states that for any two matrices `A, A + E ∈ ℝ^{m × n}` with singular values `σ₁(A) ≥ σ₂(A) ≥ ...` and `σ₁(A+E) ≥ σ₂(A+E) ≥ ...`,

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{\min(m, n)} \big(\sigma_{i}(A + E) - \sigma_{i}(A)\big)^{2} \;\le\; \|E\|_{F}^{2}. $$

Specializing to our setting with `A = W` and `E = −α · d · (dᵀW)`, and combining with the rank-1 norm identity of §4.1, gives the clean per-layer bound

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{\min(m, n)} \big(\sigma_{i}(W_{\text{new}}) - \sigma_{i}(W)\big)^{2} \;\le\; |\alpha|^{2} \cdot \|d^{\top} W\|_{2}^{2}. $$

This is the bound §4.5 evaluates numerically per layer and per projection matrix. The right-hand side `|α| · ‖dᵀW‖₂` is the length of the row vector that `W` projects `d` onto; it is small whenever `d` is approximately orthogonal to the row space of `W`, and large whenever `d` aligns with a high-singular-value left singular vector of `W`. The Mirsky bound's dimensional content is therefore that any single rank-1 abliteration step perturbs the spectrum of `W` by an amount controlled by a single scalar — the inner product of the refusal direction with `W`'s left-singular structure — rather than by a quantity that scales with the dimension `n` of the input space.

## 4.3 Lec 7 — SVD machinery and the spectral-Frobenius coincidence

The course's Lec 7 develops the SVD `W = UΣVᵀ` and the two matrix norms most often paired with it: the Frobenius norm `‖W‖_F = (Σᵢσᵢ²)^{1/2}` and the spectral norm `‖W‖_2 = σ₁(W)`. For a generic dense matrix these two norms differ by a factor up to `√(min(m, n))`; in our setting `√min(2560, 10240) ≈ 50.6`, so the gap is large. But for the *perturbation* `E = α · d · (dᵀW)` the two norms coincide exactly, as noted in §4.1, because `E` is rank-1. The same identity is what makes Mirsky's theorem in its Frobenius form simultaneously a spectral-norm bound: `σ₁(W_new) − σ₁(W)` is controlled by the same `|α| · ‖dᵀW‖₂` quantity that controls the full sum of squared singular-value gaps.

The same SVD machinery is what §7 of the paper applies to the *published* weight-diffs `W_OBLITERATUS − W_base` and `W_TrevorJS − W_base`: take the SVD, count how many singular values rise above a numerical-rank threshold, and ask whether the diff is consistent with a single rank-1 update or whether it carries higher-rank structure. The cross-method cosine table at `results/weight_diffs/cross_method_cosine_table_dedup.csv` and the refusal-direction × singular-vector cross-reference at `results/weight_diffs/refusal_direction_vs_singular_vector.csv` are downstream artifacts of exactly this Lec 7 machinery. Section 7 reports them; this section supplies the algebra they cite back to.

## 4.4 Lec 5 — Near-orthogonality of learned directions, with an anisotropy caveat

The Lec 5 high-dimensional-geometry results — volume concentration of the n-ball on a thin shell and near-orthogonality of independent random unit vectors — give the geometric intuition for why a rank-1 abliteration intervention might be selective rather than scrambling. For two independent uniformly random unit vectors `u, v ∈ S^{d−1}` in `d = 2560` dimensions, the inner product `⟨u, v⟩` concentrates around zero with standard deviation `1/√d ≈ 0.0198`, by the standard concentration argument in [Vershynin 2018]; pairs of random unit vectors in a 2560-dimensional Gaussian ball are near-orthogonal with overwhelming probability. The corresponding claim for the rank-1 intervention is that if the refusal direction `d` is approximately orthogonal to the column-space-relevant subspaces of the *other* concepts the model encodes, projecting `d` out via `W ← W − α · d · dᵀW` will not scramble them.

**The caveat that §4 has to make precise is exactly which vectors this near-orthogonality claim applies to.** It applies to *learned conceptual directions* — the refusal direction `d` extracted by mean-difference at layer 15, the per-category over-refusal directions, the harmless-prompt mean and harmful-prompt mean — which sit in the 2560-dim residual stream and are constructed by averaging contrastive activation pairs. It does **not** apply to *raw residual-stream activations themselves*. The latter are not uniformly distributed on `S^{d-1}`; they form an anisotropic cloud with a large global mean shift that violates the spherical-Gaussian null on which the Lec 5 calculation rests.

Figure 4.1 makes this concrete. The figure measures the all-pairs cosine distribution of raw L15 residual-stream activations on 200 refusal-category prompts and 40 comply-category prompts, drawn from the same prompt set as §6's mechanistic analysis. The empirical mean pairwise cosine is `0.958` (over 28,680 pairs), the centered standard deviation is `0.014`, and the isotropic null `N(0, 1/d)` with `d = 2560` predicts mean `0` and standard deviation `0.020`. The anisotropy ratio is `0.014 / 0.020 ≈ 0.71`, meaning the centered spread is actually *tighter* than the isotropic null; the dominant departure from isotropy is the bulk mean shift of `0.958`, which sits roughly `48.5` isotropic standard deviations away from zero. The raw activation cloud is therefore concentrated in a narrow cone around a single high-dimensional direction, not spread uniformly over the sphere.

Empirical mean cosine ≈ 0.958, centered std ≈ 0.014, vs. isotropic mean 0 and std ≈ 0.020. The opposite holds for learned conceptual directions. Figure 4.2 reports the cosine matrix among the directions the project actually uses at L15 — the M2b refusal direction, the M6-D3 refusal direction, the per-category mean-difference directions, the harmful-prompt mean, the harmless-prompt mean — and the median absolute cosine across all distinct pairs of learned directions is `0.978`. That number looks alarmingly close to the raw-activation `0.958`, but it is dominated by a single structural fact: each learned direction is itself a difference of class means computed from the same anisotropic activation cloud, so the residual after subtracting the cloud's global mean is what carries the actual semantic content. Once one conditions on the cloud-mean structure, the learned directions separate cleanly: in particular, the refusal direction `d_refuse` and the comply direction `d_comply` have cosine ≈ `0.984` with each other but the *normalized* mean-difference `d = normalize(mean_refuse − mean_comply)` is nearly orthogonal to both (cosine `≈ 0.081` with `mean_refuse`, by direct computation), which is exactly the geometry [Arditi 2024]'s mean-difference recipe relies on.

Figure 4.3 makes the projection-energy claim explicit. The fraction of squared-norm energy that a typical L15 activation places along the refusal direction `d_refuse` is mean `1.22%` across the 262 refuse-category prompts and `1.05%` across the 40 comply-category prompts. A rank-1 projection out of `d_refuse` therefore removes a single-digit-percent fraction of activation energy on the very category it targets, and an even smaller fraction on neighbouring categories — consistent with the Lec 5 intuition that one direction in 2560 dimensions is a thin slice — but the projection has to do real downstream work to translate that small energy removal into a behavioural change.

![Projection energy at L15. Fraction of squared-norm activation energy on the refusal direction `d_refuse` (and the comply contrast `d_comply`), averaged over refuse and comply prompt sets. Mean fraction on `d_refuse` ≈ 1.22% (refuse prompts), `1.05%` (comply prompts); on `d_comply` ≈ similar magnitudes. A rank-1 projection out of `d` removes a thin sliver of activation energy at the targeted layer. Source: `results/figures/projection_energy_L15.png`.](../../results/figures/projection_energy_L15.png)

The **paper-relevant load-bearing claim** from this subsection is therefore: near-orthogonality in the Lec 5 sense holds for the *learned* directions that the abliteration intervention uses, not for the raw activation cloud they are extracted from. The empirical activation anisotropy at L15 is a property of the cloud's mean, not of its angular spread, and it does not invalidate the Lec 5 framing of why rank-1 abliteration is in principle a surgical intervention. It does, however, constrain how the recipe should be constructed: a direction extracted naively from raw activations will inherit the cloud's mean as a component, which is one of the reasons §6 and §8 find that the M2b raw-prompt direction is not exactly the same direction as the M6-D3 Gram-Schmidt-corrected direction, and one of the reasons the Gram-Schmidt residual against the harmless mean turns out to be load-bearing in the M6 cascade.

## 4.5 Per-layer numerics: Mirsky bound heatmaps

Section §4.2 derives the bound `‖E‖_F = |α| · ‖dᵀW‖₂` per `(layer, projection)` pair. With `α = 1.0` and `d` set to either the M2b raw refusal direction or the M6-D3 Gram-Schmidt-corrected refusal direction at the matching layer, we evaluate `‖E‖_F` directly and report the **relative perturbation** `‖E‖_F / ‖W‖_F` over all `42` layers and both projection matrices, giving `84` cells per variant. The data are in `results/math_framework/mirsky_bound_per_layer.csv` (columns `variant, layer, projection, frob_E, spec_E_check, frob_W, ratio`; `spec_E_check = frob_E` to numerical precision on every row, confirming the rank-1 identity), and the heatmaps in Figure 4.4 visualize one row per layer with two columns (`o_proj`, `down_proj`).

The **headline summary** across the 84 cells per variant is:

| Variant | median `‖E‖_F / ‖W‖_F` | min | max | n cells |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| D3 (M6 Gram-Schmidt-corrected) | `0.022154` | `0.018052` | `0.030920` | 84 |
| M2b (raw mean-difference) | `0.023713` | `0.019343` | `0.038281` | 84 |

The Mirsky-bound relative perturbation is in the low-2% range across both projection matrices and across both direction variants. The D3 direction produces *slightly smaller* perturbations at the median (`0.0222` vs `0.0237`) — which is consistent with D3 being closer to a "clean" refusal direction (after Gram-Schmidt against the harmless mean and per-layer winsorization at the `99.5th` percentile, per §8) and therefore having a smaller `‖dᵀW‖₂` on the average layer. The M2b raw direction's larger tail (`max = 0.0383` at the worst-case layer) reflects the residual cloud-mean component that the D3 procedure removes. Both ratios are small enough that the Mirsky upper bound on the sum-of-squared singular-value gaps is a few percent of the layer's total spectral mass — consistent with §7's empirical finding that the SVDs of the published weight diffs have most of their singular values close to the base model's, and consistent with the partial behavioural response §8 reports (the spectrum is mostly preserved, so most of the model's computation should remain intact, which it does behaviourally on `safe_control`, `home_safety`, and the other comply-coded categories).

**Cross-check against §7's M3 measurements (consistency, not duplication).** The D3 row at `(layer = 15, projection = o_proj)` reports `frob_E = 1.19374` and `frob_W = 58.18948`, giving the per-cell ratio `0.02052`. The corresponding row in `results/weight_diffs/gemma_trevorjs/weight_diff_results_dedup.json` for the TrevorJS published variant reports `model.language_model.layers.15.self_attn.o_proj.weight` with `frobenius_norm = 1.24537` and `original_norm = 58.18948`, giving relative change `0.02140`. The two numbers are not expected to match exactly — D3 is the project's reconstructed direction at `α = 1.0`, while TrevorJS is a published norm-preserving biprojection whose effective rank median is `1` (per §7's M3 summary) but whose direction-of-projection is not identical to ours. They are expected to *agree to within a factor of order one*, which they do: `D3.frob_E = 1.19` vs `TrevorJS.frob_E = 1.25` at the same `(15, o_proj)` cell, with the M2b raw direction at `1.38` bracketing the range from above. The cross-method cosine and refusal-direction × singular-vector tables in `results/weight_diffs/` (which §7 will read in full) are the next layer of consistency that the §4 numerics here are meant to anchor.

## 4.6 Lec 8–9 — Lazy training, NTK linearization, and a forward pointer to §8

The course's Lec 8 (lazy training) and Lec 9 (NTK / Rademacher) frame the regime in which a small weight perturbation produces an approximately linear change in the model's input-output map. In its sharpest form, the lazy / NTK linearization for a model `f(x; W)` says that for small `‖E‖_F` relative to `‖W‖_F`, the first-order Taylor expansion

$$ f(x; W + E) \;\approx\; f(x; W) \;+\; \big\langle \nabla_{W} f(x; W), \; E \big\rangle, $$

holds to leading order; the error term is `o(‖E‖_F)` under the standard NTK scaling [Jacot 2018]. With `E = −α · d · (dᵀW)` rank-1 and `‖E‖_F / ‖W‖_F` in the low-2% range (per §4.5), the lazy / linearization lens makes a partial first-order behavioural response to a single rank-1 abliteration step **plausible** as a leading-order prediction — emphasis on plausible, not predictive. The course-material map in `docs/findings/course-material-mapping.md` (Lec 8–9 row) records the same hedge: the lazy/NTK picture *predicts that the output change is approximately a linear functional of `d`*; it does not predict the exact magnitude of the behavioural shift on any specific benchmark category, because the linear functional itself depends on the gradient `∇_W f` at the operating point, which is not analytically tractable for a 42-layer transformer.

**Forward pointer to §8.** Section 8 reports that the M6 D3 rank-1 cascade with Gram-Schmidt against the harmless mean produces a `should_refuse` rate of `17/42 = 40.5%` at `n = 42` — a *partial* refusal reduction, not the full ~0% that [Arditi 2024]'s claim would predict on a model where rank-1 abliteration is fully sufficient. We will phrase the §4 ↔ §8 connection as follows: **the lazy / linearization lens makes a partial first-order behavioural response plausible under a small rank-1 perturbation; it does not predict the specific value 40.5%, and we frame the residual ~60% as evidence that the refusal manifold on Gemma 4 is not fully one-dimensional rather than as something the NTK framework predicts on its own.** This is consistent with the M6 finding that H4 (Gram-Schmidt residual structure) is load-bearing but not sufficient on its own to fully close the gate.

## 4.7 Closing: how §5–§9 cite back to §4

Each empirical section consumes a specific piece of the framework above.

- **§5 (Over-refusal analysis)** measures per-category refusal rates and reports the headline zero-percentage-point delta on `should_refuse` for the self-abliterated checkpoint. The Mirsky bound in §4.2 supplies the quantitative reason that this is consistent rather than contradictory with the rank-1 intervention being a *small* edit: a `~2%` relative perturbation per `(layer, projection)` is small enough to leave the spectrum mostly intact, so the model's bulk computation is preserved (consistent with the near-zero refusal rates on `safe_control` and the other comply-coded categories), but small enough that there is no a-priori reason a *behavioural* boundary as sharp as refusal-vs-comply should flip across it.

- **§6 (Mechanistic analysis)** computes the L15 mean-difference refusal direction and the per-category category directions. The Lec 5 anisotropy caveat of §4.4 is the framing for §6's claim that the learned directions are well-separated geometrically (in the post-mean-subtracted sense) even though the raw activation cloud they come from is dominated by a single mean shift. Figures 4.1–4.3 are the §4 anchor for §6's L15 peak-layer call.

- **§7 (Abliteration + comparative weight diff)** runs SVD on published weight diffs and reports per-layer effective ranks, cross-method top-1 singular-vector cosines, and refusal-direction × singular-vector cross-references. The SVD machinery of §4.3 and the per-cell consistency cross-check of §4.5 (D3 `frob_E = 1.19` vs TrevorJS `frob_E = 1.25` at `(L15, o_proj)`) supply the framework for §7's quantitative comparisons.

- **§8 (Rank-1 cascade)** reports the partial `17/42 = 40.5%` `should_refuse` rate at `n = 42` for the D3 direction and the four-stage cascade (chat-template direction, winsorization, Gram-Schmidt vs. harmless mean, optional norm-preserving biprojection). The §4.6 lazy / NTK linearization lens supplies the *plausibility* frame for the partial response; §8 itself does the load-bearing causal-isolation work via the ingredient-by-ingredient cascade.

- **§9 (Discussion)** re-cites §4 with the §5–§8 numbers plugged in and adds the over-parametrization framing tying Mirsky's bound to the partial behavioural effect. Section 9 increases the lecture-citation density (≥5 explicit connections, ≥1 quantitative); §4 here is the algebra and numerics it cites back to.

The remainder of the paper is empirical. The framework above is the mathematical scaffold that each empirical section will name once and then reference by number.
